LCP deep dive: why your largest element is slower than you think

11 min read · February 2025

Largest Contentful Paint gets misunderstood more than any other Core Web Vital. Teams spend weeks optimizing images and then wonder why their LCP score barely moved. Usually it's because they were optimizing the wrong element.

What actually counts as the LCP element

The browser considers: images, <img> elements, <image> inside SVGs, poster images on <video>, background images loaded via CSS, and blocks of text. What it does NOT consider: SVG elements, <canvas>, or anything with opacity: 0.

The LCP element can change during load. The browser keeps updating the candidate until the user first interacts — then it locks in. This means your LCP element might not be what you assume. Check it in DevTools, not in your head.

The four sub-parts of LCP

Chrome's LCP breakdown gives you four phases: Time to First Byte, resource load delay, resource load time, and element render delay. Most teams only look at the total. That's why they keep missing the actual bottleneck. 

// Log LCP candidate in JS
new PerformanceObserver((list) => {
  const entries = list.getEntries();
  const last = entries[entries.length - 1];
  console.log('LCP element:', last.element);
  console.log('LCP time:', last.startTime);
}).observe({ type: 'largest-contentful-paint', buffered: true });

The fastest fix most teams ignore

If your LCP element is an image, add <link rel="preload" as="image"> for it in the <head>. This is free performance. The browser discovers the image earlier in the load waterfall and starts fetching it before it parses the full DOM. It's not glamorous but it consistently cuts 200–400ms off LCP on image-heavy pages.
