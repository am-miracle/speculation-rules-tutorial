Font loading strategies that don't destroy your CLS

7 min read · November 2024

Custom fonts are one of the most reliable ways to tank your Cumulative Layout Shift score. Text renders in a fallback font, the custom font loads, the text reflows, and CLS spikes. There are several ways to prevent this, and most teams are using the wrong one.

font-display explained properly

font-display: swap is the most commonly recommended value, but it's not always the right one. Swap shows fallback text immediately and swaps in the custom font when it loads — which is great for FOIT (flash of invisible text) but can cause visible layout shift. For body text, it's often the right trade-off. For display headings where CLS matters more, consider font-display: optional instead. 

@font-face {
  font-family: 'MyFont';
  src: url('/fonts/myfont.woff2') format('woff2');
  font-display: swap; /* or: optional, fallback, block */
}

size-adjust and ascent-override

These CSS descriptors let you tweak the metrics of your fallback font to match your custom font more closely — reducing the visual jump when the swap happens. It's a bit tedious to calibrate, but tools like the Font Style Matcher and the @font-face override descriptors make it much more manageable.

Self-hosting vs Google Fonts

Google Fonts is convenient but it adds a cross-origin connection and gives you less control over caching. Self-hosting your fonts — combined with font-display: optional and aggressive caching headers — consistently beats Google Fonts on performance benchmarks. The setup is a one-time cost that pays off on every page load.
